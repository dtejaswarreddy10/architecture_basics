Introduction

What is an FPGA?

An FPGA (Field-Programmable Gate Array) is a chip made of reconfigurable hardware. Instead of running software on a fixed processor, you rewire the chip itself to become the circuit you want — a network controller, a video processor, an AI accelerator, anything. A CPU/GPU runs instructions on fixed hardware; an FPGA lets you change the hardware to match the problem.

Where did FPGAs come from?

FPGAs emerged in the mid-1980s (Xilinx shipped the first in 1985) as a flexible middle ground between fixed custom chips (ASICs) — fast but unchangeable — and general processors — flexible but slower for specialized tasks. They became essential for prototyping chips and for small-volume custom hardware.

The main idea behind FPGA design

Make the hardware itself programmable — a sea of generic logic blocks and wires, configured by a bitstream so the same physical chip can implement any digital design and be reprogrammed later.

Basic Functionality & Architecture

Simple analogy: electronic LEGO

An FPGA is a giant box of electronic LEGO. The blocks are generic, but you snap them into almost any shape — and if you want something different tomorrow, you take it apart and rebuild.

Applications

Where FPGAs shine

🔬 Chip prototyping / emulation 🌐 Networking & smart NICs 📈 High-frequency trading 📡 Telecom & 5G 🛰️ Aerospace & defense 🎥 Video & broadcast 🤖 Custom accelerators

✅ Pick an FPGA for

  • Custom hardware for one task
  • Deterministic ultra-low latency
  • Fine-grained parallelism
  • Specs that may change

⛔ Not good at

  • General software / OS → CPU
  • Easy programming (HDL is hard)
  • Lowest cost at huge volume → ASIC
  • Peak dense matrix math → GPU/TPU

Summary

FPGA in one paragraph

An FPGA is a chip full of reconfigurable logic blocks that you wire into any digital circuit using an HDL. Because the design runs as real parallel hardware, FPGAs deliver low, predictable latency and high efficiency while staying reprogrammable — a flexible middle ground between general processors and fixed ASICs. Ideal for prototyping, networking, real-time processing, and evolving specs.

Choose an FPGA when…

  • You need custom hardware, not millions of units
  • Latency must be ultra-low & deterministic
  • Your design may change
  • You're prototyping a chip

Future trends

  • Adaptive SoCs (CPU + AI engines + fabric)
  • FPGAs as data-center accelerators
  • Easier design via High-Level Synthesis
  • Power-efficient FPGAs for edge AI
