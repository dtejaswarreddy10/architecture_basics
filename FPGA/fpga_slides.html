1 What is an FPGA?

An FPGA (Field-Programmable Gate Array) is a chip made of reconfigurable hardware. Instead of running software on a fixed processor, you rewire the chip itself to become the circuit you want — a network controller, a video processor, an AI accelerator, anything.

Make the hardware itself programmable — a sea of generic logic blocks and wires, configured by a bitstream so one physical chip can implement any digital design and be reprogrammed later.

The key difference

A CPU/GPU runs instructions on fixed hardware; an FPGA lets you change the hardware itself to match the problem.

2 Where FPGAs came from

FPGAs emerged in the mid-1980s as a flexible middle ground between fixed custom chips (ASICs) — fast but unchangeable — and general processors — flexible but slower for specialized tasks.

They became essential for prototyping chips and for small-volume custom hardware.

Analogy: electronic LEGO

An FPGA is a giant box of electronic LEGO — generic blocks you snap into almost any shape, then take apart and rebuild when you want something different tomorrow.

Synthesizemap to LUTs, DSPs, BRAM

6 Applications

FPGAs shine wherever you need custom, low-latency hardware:

🔬 Chip prototyping 🌐 Networking & smart NICs 📈 High-frequency trading 📡 Telecom & 5G 🛰️ Aerospace & defense 🎥 Video & broadcast 🤖 Custom accelerators

✅ Pick an FPGA for

  • Custom hardware for one task
  • Deterministic ultra-low latency
  • Fine-grained parallelism
  • Specs that may change

⛔ Not good at

  • General software / OS → CPU
  • Easy programming (HDL is hard)
  • Lowest cost at huge volume → ASIC
  • Dense matrix math → GPU/TPU

8 FPGA in one paragraph

An FPGA is a chip full of reconfigurable logic blocks that you wire into any digital circuit using an HDL. Because the design runs as real parallel hardware, FPGAs deliver low, predictable latency and high efficiency while staying reprogrammable — a flexible middle ground between general processors and fixed ASICs.

Choose an FPGA when…

  • You need custom hardware, not millions of units
  • Latency must be ultra-low & deterministic
  • Your design may change
  • You're prototyping a chip

Future trends

  • Adaptive SoCs (CPU + AI engines + fabric)
  • Data-center accelerators
  • Easier design via High-Level Synthesis
  • Power-efficient FPGAs for edge AI
