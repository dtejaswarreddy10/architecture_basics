Introduction

What is a DSP?

A DSP (Digital Signal Processor) is a specialized microprocessor optimized for math on signals — continuous streams like sound, radio waves, images, and sensor readings. It applies mathematical operations (filtering, transforms) to these streams extremely fast and in real time. A CPU is built for general logic; a DSP is built for repetitive math on streaming data.

Where did DSPs come from?

DSPs arose in the late 1970s–80s when engineers needed to process signals digitally instead of with bulky analog circuits. Early wins were modems, digital telephony, and audio. Texas Instruments' TMS320 family (1983) made DSPs mainstream.

The main idea behind DSP design

Do multiply-accumulate (MAC) as fast as physically possible — the whole architecture (memory, datapath, instructions) is shaped around streaming data through MAC units without stalls.

Basic Functionality & Architecture

Simple analogy: an assembly line for signals

A DSP is a conveyor belt where samples flow in one end, get multiplied and summed at each station, and stream out the other end — continuously, predictably, with no pauses.

Applications

Where DSPs are everywhere

🎧 Audio & hearing aids 📱 Mobile / 5G baseband 📡 Communications 🎥 Image & video 🚗 Automotive radar 🏥 Medical imaging 🎙️ Voice front-ends

✅ DSP over CPU when

  • Real-time guarantees on streams
  • Far better power efficiency
  • Single-cycle MAC & streaming memory
  • Cheaper than CPU+GPU for signals

⛔ Not good at

  • General computing / UIs → CPU
  • Large-model AI throughput → GPU/TPU
  • Complex branching / OS → CPU
  • Non-signal-shaped tasks

Summary

DSP in one paragraph

A DSP is a processor built to crunch real-world signals in real time, organized entirely around the multiply-accumulate operation. With single-cycle MACs, Harvard memory, hardware loops, and deterministic timing, it filters and transforms audio, radio, and sensor data far more efficiently than a general CPU. Today DSP cores live inside almost every SoC and are steadily merging with AI engines for low-power edge inference.

Choose a DSP when…

  • You process continuous signals in real time
  • You need deterministic timing & low power
  • The work is MAC-heavy (filters, FFTs)
  • It's embedded / always-on

Future trends

  • DSP + AI convergence (TinyML)
  • Always-on, ultra-low-power blocks
  • DSP cores embedded in every SoC
  • Vector DSPs blurring into NPUs
