1 What is a DSP?

A DSP (Digital Signal Processor) is a specialized microprocessor optimized for math on signals — continuous streams like sound, radio waves, images, and sensor readings. It applies filtering and transforms extremely fast and in real time.

Do multiply-accumulate (MAC) as fast as physically possible — the whole architecture is shaped around streaming data through MAC units without stalls.

The main idea

A CPU is built for general logic; a DSP is built for repetitive math on streaming data. Every part of the chip exists to keep the MAC units fed, cycle after cycle.

2 Where DSPs came from

DSPs arose when engineers needed to process signals digitally instead of with bulky analog circuits.

1983
TI TMS320 · went mainstream
1970s–80s
DSPs first emerged

Early wins were modems, digital telephony, and audio. Texas Instruments' TMS320 family made DSPs mainstream.

Analogy: an assembly line for signals

A DSP is a conveyor belt where samples flow in one end, get multiplied and summed at each station, and stream out the other — continuously, predictably, with no pauses.

3 The core operation: MAC

At its heart sits one operation, repeated millions of times. Each output sample flows through it:

Multiplyx[n] × h[k]
Accumulate+ Σ running sum
Outputy[n] sample

One MAC per clock cycle (often several in parallel). This single pattern — a FIR filter — appears everywhere in signal processing.

  • Single-cycle MAC & streaming memory

8 DSP in one paragraph

A DSP is a processor built to crunch real-world signals in real time, organized entirely around the multiply-accumulate operation. With single-cycle MACs, Harvard memory, hardware loops, and deterministic timing, it filters and transforms audio, radio, and sensor data far more efficiently than a general CPU. Today DSP cores live inside almost every SoC and are steadily merging with AI engines for low-power edge inference.

Choose a DSP when…

  • Continuous signals in real time
  • Deterministic timing & low power
  • MAC-heavy work (filters, FFTs)
  • Embedded / always-on

Future trends

  • DSP + AI convergence (TinyML)
  • Always-on, ultra-low-power blocks
  • DSP cores in every SoC
  • Vector DSPs blurring into NPUs
